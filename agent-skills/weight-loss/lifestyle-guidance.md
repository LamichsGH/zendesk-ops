# Weight Loss Lifestyle Guidance

## Scope

This file covers general lifestyle, nutrition, exercise, mindset, and plateau guidance for weight loss patients. All responses are general information only — never personalised clinical advice.

**Covers:**
- Balanced nutrition principles (Balanced Plate model, hydration, GLP-1-specific tips)
- Exercise and movement guidance (cardio, resistance training, NEAT)
- Plateau reassurance and non-scale victories
- Mindset, motivation, and emotional eating strategies
- Habit formation principles

- Clinical side effects from medication → see `side-effects.md`

### Nutrition Questions

  → confidence = 0.85 (SOLVE)

"Here are some general nutrition principles that many people find helpful during their weight loss journey:

**The Balanced Plate Model:**
- 1/2 plate: non-starchy vegetables (for volume and fibre)
- 1/4 plate: lean protein — chicken, fish, eggs, tofu, beans (for fullness and muscle health)
- 1/4 plate: complex carbohydrates — wholegrain rice, pasta, bread, sweet potato (for sustained energy)
- Small amounts of healthy fats (olive oil, avocado, nuts)

**General Tips:**
- Prioritise protein at each meal — it helps you feel full and preserves muscle during weight loss
- Stay hydrated — aim for around 2 litres of water per day
- Eat slowly and mindfully to recognise fullness cues
- Focus on whole, minimally processed foods where possible
- Avoid labelling foods as 'good' or 'bad' — balance is key

**GLP-1-Specific Tips:**
- Smaller, more frequent meals can help if you experience reduced appetite or nausea
- Eat protein first at each meal — it's easier to eat when you're hungriest
- Rich or fatty foods may be harder to tolerate — lighter options often work better
- Hydration is particularly important on GLP-1 medication

*This is general guidance only and isn't a substitute for personalised medical advice. For advice specific to your health, please speak to your clinician.*"

### Exercise Questions

IF patient asks about exercise, movement, working out, or physical activity:
  → confidence = 0.85 (SOLVE)

"Here are some evidence-based movement principles that support weight loss and overall health:

**General Guidance:**
- **Moderate activity**: Aim for approximately 150 minutes of moderate-intensity activity per week — brisk walking, cycling, swimming, or dancing all count
- **Resistance training**: 2-3 sessions per week helps preserve muscle during weight loss. You don't need a gym — bodyweight exercises (squats, push-ups, lunges) or resistance bands work well
- **NEAT (Non-Exercise Activity Thermogenesis)**: Daily movement outside of structured exercise makes a real difference. Taking stairs, walking to the shops, standing more — it all adds up
- **Start gradually**: If you're new to exercise, begin with what feels manageable and build up over time. Any movement is better than none

**Tips:**
- Find activities you enjoy — sustainability matters more than intensity
- Rest and recovery days are important
- Walking is one of the most accessible and effective forms of exercise
- Listen to your body, especially when starting or increasing medication doses

*This is general guidance only and isn't a substitute for personalised medical advice. For advice specific to your health, please speak to your clinician.*"

### Plateau Questions

IF patient is worried about a weight loss plateau or stall:
  → confidence = 0.85 (SOLVE)

"Weight loss plateaus are very common and are usually a normal part of the process — not a sign that something is wrong. Here's what to know:

**Why Plateaus Happen:**
- Your body naturally adapts during weight loss — metabolism adjusts, water retention fluctuates, and hormonal shifts occur
- Plateaus lasting 2-4 weeks are completely normal
- Daily weight can fluctuate by 1-2kg due to water, sodium, hormones, and digestion

**Non-Scale Victories to Focus On:**
- How your clothes fit
- Energy levels and mood
- Strength and fitness improvements
- Better sleep quality
- Measurements (waist, hips) may be changing even when weight isn't

**General Tips During a Plateau:**
- Stay consistent with your current habits — consistency matters more than perfection
- Review portion sizes, as they can gradually increase over time without noticing
- Ensure you're getting enough protein and staying hydrated
- Keep moving — even if the scale isn't budging, exercise is supporting your health
- Don't panic — your body is adjusting, and progress will resume

If your plateau lasts more than 4-6 weeks and you're concerned, your clinician can review your treatment plan.

*This is general guidance only and isn't a substitute for personalised medical advice. For advice specific to your health, please speak to your clinician.*"

### Mindset, Motivation, and Emotional Eating

IF patient asks about motivation, emotional eating, mindset, or setbacks:
  → confidence = 0.85 (SOLVE)

"Mindset is a really important part of any health journey, and it's completely normal to have ups and downs. Here are some general strategies that many people find helpful:

**Setbacks and Motivation:**
- Setbacks don't erase progress — they're a normal part of any journey, not a reason to give up
- Progress isn't linear — weight fluctuates, energy varies, and motivation comes in waves
- Self-compassion is more effective than self-criticism. Be kind to yourself
- Focus on building sustainable habits rather than chasing perfection

**Emotional Eating:**
- Awareness is the first step — try keeping a brief food diary noting what you ate and how you felt
- Create a pause before eating: ask yourself 'Am I physically hungry, or is this an emotional response?'
- Identify your triggers — stress, boredom, tiredness, and loneliness are common ones
- There's no judgment around food. Food serves many purposes, and understanding your patterns takes time
- Having alternative coping strategies ready can help — a walk, calling a friend, a cup of tea, journalling

**Habit Formation:**
- Start with one small, specific change at a time
- Consistency beats intensity — doing something small every day is more effective than doing something big occasionally
- Habits take time to form — be patient with yourself
- Attach new habits to existing routines (e.g., 'after my morning coffee, I'll take a 10-minute walk')

If you feel you need more in-depth support with emotional eating or motivation, our clinical team can help. Just reply to this email and we'll connect you.

*This is general guidance only and isn't a substitute for personalised medical advice. For advice specific to your health, please speak to your clinician.*"

**Example 2: Exercise question from a beginner**

Patient: "I want to start exercising but I don't know where to begin. I haven't been active in years."

→ confidence = 0.85 (SOLVE)

"Starting from scratch is completely normal, and the most important thing is to begin at a pace that feels manageable for you. Here are some tips:

- **Aim for 150 minutes of moderate activity per week** as a long-term goal, but there's no rush to get there.
- **Try to add resistance training** 2-3 times a week when you feel ready. This doesn't require a gym — squats, wall push-ups, and using resistance bands at home all work well. Resistance training helps preserve muscle during weight loss.

The key is consistency over intensity — doing something small regularly is far more effective than doing something intense once in a while.

- **Create a pause**: When you feel the urge to eat in the evening, try pausing for 5-10 minutes. Ask yourself: 'Am I physically hungry, or is this a stress response?' Either answer is okay — it's about building awareness.

- **Don't restrict during the day**: If you're under-eating during the day, evening bingeing can be your body's way of catching up. Make sure you're eating regular, balanced meals.
- **Be compassionate**: Beating yourself up tends to make the cycle worse, not better. One difficult evening doesn't define your journey.
